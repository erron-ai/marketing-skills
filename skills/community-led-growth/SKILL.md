# Community-Led Growth

You help teams design and grow product communities — building engaged spaces where customers help each other, advocate for the product, and accelerate growth through word of mouth.

## Before You Start

Read `.agents/product-marketing-context.md`. Community-led growth works best when the product solves a problem that practitioners care deeply about and want to discuss with peers.

## When Community-Led Growth Makes Sense

Community is worth investing in when:
- Your ICP already congregates in professional communities (Slack groups, subreddits, forums)
- Users gain value by connecting with other users (shared workflows, peer benchmarking)
- Your product category is evolving fast and practitioners want to stay current
- Customer success is peer-driven (users help each other before contacting support)

Community is a long game — plan for 6–12 months before seeing meaningful acquisition from it.

## Step-by-Step Process

### 1. Define the Community's Purpose

A thriving community is built around a shared identity, not a product. Answer:

- **Who is this for?** (Job title, stage, aspiration)
- **What do they come to do?** (Learn, get help, network, share wins, vent)
- **What's the core identity?** ("I'm a [job title] who cares about [topic]")
- **What's the product's role?** (Host, facilitator, or just one of many tools they discuss)

The community name and positioning should reflect the member identity, not the company brand. Example: "Growth Operators" not "[Product] Users Group."

### 2. Choose the Right Platform

| Platform | Best For | Size Sweet Spot | Moderation Effort |
|----------|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Slack | Real-time discussion, async threads | 100–5,000 | Medium |
| Discord | Developer and enthusiast communities | 500–50,000+ | High |
| Circle | Courses + community, structured content | 50–5,000 | Low-Medium |
| Reddit | Open discovery, anonymous discussion | 1,000+ | Low-Medium |
| LinkedIn Group | B2B professional communities | 500–10,000 | Low |
| Forum (Discourse) | Long-form technical discussion | 200–100,000+ | Medium |

### 3. Design the Core Channels/Categories

Keep structure minimal at launch. Add channels only when there's clear demand. Starting set:

**For a Slack/Discord community:**
- `#introductions` — new member onboarding
- `#general` — catch-all discussion
- `#[primary-topic]` — the core conversation
- `#wins` or `#show-and-tell` — celebrate member achievements
- `#resources` — curated links, templates, tools
- `#help` — questions and peer support
- `#[product]-feedback` — product discussion (optional, keep it secondary)

### 4. Seed the Community (0–100 Members)

Before opening to the public:
- Invite 20–30 existing power users personally; explain why you want them involved
- Brief them on the community purpose and expectations
- Seed 2 weeks of content before the first outsider joins
- Create a "founding member" badge or recognition
- Ask each founding member to bring 2–3 peers

In this phase: quality of conversation matters more than quantity of members. A dead community with 500 members is worse than a lively one with 50.

### 5. Community Flywheel Design

The flywheel turns strangers into contributors:

```
Discover → Join → Get value → Contribute → Invite others → [cycle repeats]
```

Design interventions at each stage:

| Stage | Friction | Intervention |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Discover | Hard to find | SEO-optimized landing page, mention in product onboarding, social posts |
| Join | Signup friction | One-click join, clear value prop on invite page |
| Get value | Silence after joining | Welcome DM, automated introduction prompt, curated first week digest |
| Contribute | Unsure what to share | Weekly prompts, explicit calls to "share your take," ask questions to specific members |
| Invite others | No incentive | Founding member perks, referral recognition, "bring a colleague" campaigns |

### 6. Community Programs

Programs structure recurring value and give members a reason to stay active:

**AMAs**: Monthly expert session with a practitioner outside the company
**Weekly prompts**: A discussion question posted every Monday
**Member spotlights**: Feature a member's work or story monthly
**Resource drops**: Curated template, checklist, or guide released weekly
**Challenges**: Time-boxed activity (e.g., "audit your analytics stack this week")
**Office hours**: Live Q&A with your team

### 7. Measure Community Health

| Metric | Healthy Range |
|--------|--------------|
| Monthly active members / total members | > 30% |
| Questions answered within 24h | > 80% |
| New member retention at 30 days | > 50% |
| Messages per active member per month | > 5 |
| Referral-sourced pipeline (if tracking) | > 10% of community-sourced leads |

Review monthly. A declining D30 retention is the earliest warning sign.

### 8. Connect Community to Revenue

Track community-sourced pipeline:
- Add UTM or referral source to community-linked signups
- Survey new customers: "How did you hear about us?" — track "from [community] member"
- Monitor if community members have shorter sales cycles or higher retention
- Build case studies from community power users
