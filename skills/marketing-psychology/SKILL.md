# Marketing Psychology

You apply behavioral science, cognitive biases, and psychological principles to specific marketing decisions — pricing, copy, design, conversion optimization, and customer communication.

## Starting Point

Load `.agents/product-marketing-context.md` for product and audience context. Apply principles in context — the same principle shows up differently in a B2B enterprise sale vs. a consumer product.

When asked, identify the specific decision or problem first, then apply the most relevant principle(s).

---

## Core Principles and Where to Apply Them

### Anchoring

People make judgments relative to the first number or reference point they see.

**Applications:**
- **Pricing**: Show the higher plan first, or show the annual price before the monthly. "$240/year" anchors higher than "$20/month" even though they're the same.
- **Features**: Lead with your highest-value feature so everything else seems like extra value.
- **Negotiation**: Name a price first in any negotiation.

### Loss Aversion

People feel losses about twice as strongly as equivalent gains.

**Applications:**
- **Trial expiry**: "Don't lose access to your 14 days of reports" outperforms "Keep your trial."
- **Paywalls**: "You'll lose [specific feature]" outperforms "Upgrade to keep using [feature]."
- **Churn prevention**: Show exactly what the customer will lose when they cancel — specific features, data, history.

### Social Proof

People look to others to determine what's correct, especially in uncertainty.

**Types and when to use:**
| Type | When most powerful |
|------|-------------------|
| Numbers ("Join 40,000+ teams") | When skepticism is high |
| Expert endorsement | When technical credibility matters |
| Peer testimonials | When the buyer relates to the person |
| Customer logos | When brand recognition matters |
| Review ratings | When third-party validation matters |

**Placement**: Social proof is most powerful near a conversion point — beside the CTA, not buried in the footer.

### Scarcity and Urgency

Limited availability or time increases perceived value and drives action.

**When authentic**: Limited seats, closing cohorts, expiring discounts, genuine deadlines.
**When it backfires**: Fake countdown timers, manufactured scarcity that anyone can see through.

**Rule**: Only create urgency when it's real. False urgency destroys trust faster than it creates conversions.

### Reciprocity

People feel obligated to return favors. Giving something of value first creates a psychological obligation to reciprocate.

**Applications:**
- Free tools, templates, and resources that genuinely help the prospect
- Free audits or reviews with real value — not a sales pitch disguised as a gift
- Personalized outreach that shares a useful insight before asking for anything

### The Paradox of Choice

More options increase cognitive load and reduce the likelihood of choosing any option.

**Applications:**
- **Pricing pages**: 3 plans max. 4 is usually one too many.
- **Navigation**: Fewer nav items increase engagement with what remains.
- **CTAs**: One primary CTA per page or section. Multiple equal CTAs reduce clicks on all of them.
- **Feature presentation**: Don't list every feature. Show the 3–5 that matter most to the buyer.

### The Peak-End Rule

People remember an experience primarily by its most intense moment and its ending — not the average.

**Applications:**
- **Onboarding**: Make the moment of first value ("aha moment") as good as possible.
- **Cancellation**: A graceful offboarding experience leaves the door open for win-back.
- **Customer success**: Celebrate milestones — the 100th email sent, the first report generated.

### The Mere Exposure Effect

Familiarity breeds preference. People tend to prefer things they've encountered before.

**Applications:**
- **Retargeting ads**: Showing your product to people who've visited your site increases preference, even before they click.
- **Newsletter presence**: Consistent newsletter delivery builds familiarity that makes eventual conversion more likely.

### Commitment and Consistency

People want to act consistently with prior commitments. Small commitments lead to larger ones.

**Applications:**
- **Progressive onboarding**: Get a small commitment first (choose your goal, name your workspace) before asking for a bigger one.
- **Micro-conversions**: A content download leads to a webinar sign-up leads to a trial.
- **Public commitments**: A user who tells their team they'll try your product is more likely to activate.

## Output Format

When applying principles to a specific problem:
1. Identify which principle(s) apply to the situation
2. Explain why the current approach may be working against the principle
3. Give 2–3 specific changes that apply the principle correctly
